Scotland's team came up with the trumps in the Snowdon International Race, Robbie Simpson (Deeside) winning with over a minute to spare from Alex Baldaccini (Italy) and Ian Holmes (England). Alasdair Anthony (Ochils) finished twelfth, Matt Sullivan (Shettleston) sixteenth and Andy Fallas (Carnethy) seventeenth.

Catriona Buchanan (Ochils) marked her first senior cap by coming home as first woman (39th overall), followed by Jill Mykura (fourth woman) and Sula Young (eighth woman).

Report from Scotland team manager, Malcolm Patterson:

“It was a great day for Scotland at the International Snowdon Race on Saturday (July 24th). Robbie Simpson, at 18 years old, became the youngest ever winner of the race, tracking his more fancied rivals Rolando Piana (Italy) and Wilson Chemweno (Kenya) to the summit and then having a storming descent to move into a lead that was never threatened. He finished in 67.59, over a minute ahead of Italy’s Alex Baldacinni and three times winner Ian Holmes of England. Equalling the achievement of her younger (by two months!) Scotland team-mate, it was Catriona Buchanan who came down out of the cloud and rain to finish as first female, having moved into the lead towards the top of the climb and, like Robbie, remaining comfortably ahead of her more experienced opponents, Pippa Maddams and Lauren Jeska of England. Jill Mykura was justifiably delighted with her fourth place, only seconds behind the English pair. Sula Young had a strong run to complete team Scotland in eighth, though she was bitterly disappointed to be just behind Jill at the summit only to lose four places on the long, wet, testing descent. Seasoned international Al Anthony was next Scot amongst the men, pleased to finish 12th but voicing his frustration that he was held back on the way down by a lack of confidence, understandable in the wake of his bad fall in Ben Lomond earlier this year. Matt Sullivan and Andy Fallas, both making their international debut and both running Snowdon for the first time, coped admirably well with the pressure to put in strong performances, coming home in 16th and 17th. Matt would surely have finished higher still, but for a bad nose bleed during the race.”
